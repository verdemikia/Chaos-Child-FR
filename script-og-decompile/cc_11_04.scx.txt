[name]Man[line]“...Finally, with the hope that Shibuya, a city reborn, will experience even greater prosperity, I conclude this speech by declaring the second Shibuya Peace and Restoration Festival open! Thank you all for your cooperation!”[%p]
November 6th, 2015. 9:00 AM.[%p]
The second Shibuya Peace and Restoration Festival had begun.[%p]
The people gathered in front of the Hikariwo began to loudly applaud when the mayor finished his speech.[%p]
Then they started to split off to visit the concerts and stalls that they wanted to go see.[%p]
Over 80% of the people here had come from outside Shibuya.[%p]
Many were from Tokyo, but others had come all the way from outside the country to witness the celebration.[%p]
There were easily more than 100,000 people attending.[%p]
The first festival was held last year, to celebrate the fifth anniversary of the reconstruction, and the whole ward had come out to celebrate.[%p]
A similar event had also been done a year after the earthquake, but it had been less of a festival and more of a memorial.[%p]
The rebuilding work was proceeding at a feverish pitch, but at that point it was still too early to call it “a city reborn.”[%p]
But last year...[%p]
...even though there were still areas that were covered in rubble, the fifth year anniversary, and the prospect of the tax revenue that would come along with it...[%p]
...were enough to get the government to hold a festival for the whole ward.[%p]
Despite being only a one-day event, it had attracted over 80,000 people, and worldwide attention.[%p]
It was such a big success that for this year, the city had put even more effort into it: the concerts were bigger and there were more places to eat and drink.[%p]
The main areas were centered around the Hikariwo building, as well as the Shibuya Cultural Hall, Shibuya’s 107 building, and Shibuya Sign City.[%p]
All the main roads around the station were closed to any traffic that wasn’t bringing in people or equipment to the festival.[%p]
Bunkamura-dori and Dogenzaka-dori in particular were overflowing with performers and stalls.[%p]
[name]Reporter[line]“Should we do this spot next?”[%p]
The reporter with Tokyo NEX TV saw the cameraman nod, and then looked at the scene in front of her. This whole thing was stupid, and she could tell she was getting tired.[%p]
Center-gai.[%p]
One of Shibuya’s busiest shopping streets.[%p]
This street always had a lot of young people to begin with, and now with the festival, it was covered with stalls to visit, and filled with people of all ages.[%p]
[name]Reporter[line](There’s too many people here...)[%p]
When she’d been given the job of reporting on the Shibuya Reconstruction Festival several months ago, she’d literally jumped for joy.[%p]
The reason she was so excited was that the vocalist for one of her favorite bands, Takayanagi Momone, was going to be performing on stage.[%p]
Ever since she’d happened to hear Takayanagi’s voice at a concert hall, she’d been a fan—or perhaps, a dedicated believer.[%p]
She was willing to do whatever it took to get a camera to her performance at the Restoration Festival, so that the whole world could see how wonderful she was.[%p]
But...[%p]
[name]Reporter[line](...Why did she have to die?)[%p]
She’d been caught up in the Return of the New Generation Madness and was murdered.[%p]
Now there was barely any point in covering the Restoration Festival anymore.[%p]
But she still had to smile and obey when her boss told her to come here. And in the end...[%p]
[name]Reporter[line](...Now I’m off chasing rumors?)[%p]
She’d wanted to believe that a big TV station would be better than this. What she was doing was little better than rubbernecking.[%p]
[name]Cameraman[line]“Oh, there they are. Look.”[%p]
The cameraman pointed at a group of people wearing homemade t-shirts. The shirts had a white print background with a photo on the front, and a big red “...?” that covered the photo.[%p]
They were all young. Just like all the others she’d interviewed so far.[%p]
[name]Reporter[line]“...”[%p]
She covered her annoyance with the same smile she used to deal with her obnoxious boss. Then she pushed through the crowd and got close to the group.[%p]
[name]Reporter[line]“Hello. What are you up to?”[%p]
[name]Man[line]“Wow, are you from TV? What station?”[%p]
“Who cares?” she thought to herself, but she told them she was with Tokyo NEX.[%p]
[name]Man[line]“This. Look.”[%p]
He pulled on his t-shirt to show her.[%p]
The photo in the center was of two fat male faces—the Sumo Sticker.[%p]
[name]Man[line]“So if the TV’s here, is it true? What they said on Twipo.”[%p]
[name]Reporter[line]“Huh? What do you mean?”[%p]
She played dumb and acted surprised.[%p]
She certainly couldn’t tell them that she’d come here because of a rumor on Twipo, especially one that was almost certainly a lie.[%p]
[name]Man[line]“There’s a rumor that Miyashiro’s going to kill himself right here.”[%p]
She knew about that. Before dawn, it was supposed to happen in front of the big TVs, and a few hours ago it was next to the statue of Hachiko in front of the station.[%p]
[name]Reporter[line]“By Miyashiro, do you mean Miyashiro Takuru-kun, the fugitive?”[%p]
[name]Man[line]“Yeah, I’ve got a picture.”[%p]
He started to fiddle with his smartphone, seemingly oblivious to the fact that she hadn’t asked to see it.[%p]
It was the same picture of Miyashiro Takuru that she was already sick of seeing—according to her boss, it was from his student handbook.[%p]
[name]Reporter[line]“What? H-How did you get this?”[%p]
[name]Man[line]“Haha, you don’t spend a lot of time on the internet, do you? Everybody’s got it.”[%p]
[name]Man[line]“We’re totally gonna get that reward.”[%p]
A reward. Come to think of it, there was a fake rumor about that going around.[%p]
[name]Reporter[line]“Did you make those t-shirts yourself?”[%p]
[name]Man[line]“No way. We just bought them for 300 yen back there. Don’t they look stupid? It’s just a meme.”[%p]
And then they all started to laugh, though she didn’t get the joke.[%p]
[name]Reporter[line]“...”[%p]
She could feel herself getting more and more aggravated.[%p]
She’d seen similar t-shirts and placards, and even some that were declaring Miyashiro Takuru a god, and glorifying the Sumo Stickers.[%p]
Some of them were being sold as part of a movement to get rid of the Sumo Stickers so people could focus on the festival, but most people were just wearing them to be a part of the chaos.[%p]
Of course, this was the first time she’d seen anyone who actually believed in the reward.[%p]
[name]Reporter[line]“What are your thoughts on Miyashiro Takuru-kun?”[%p]
[name]Man[line]“Total son of a bitch. He’s a serial killer, right? And he even killed people from his own orphanage? What a sicko.”[%p]
[name]Man[line]“Those weird otaku guys say he’s some kind of god, but they’re just a bunch of creepy losers. I hate those assholes.”[%p]
She ignored most of what they were saying.[%p]
There was no need to go any deeper. As long as she had the footage on her camera, that would be good enough.[%p]
[name]Reporter[line](But...)[%p]
Everyone seemed to think that Miyashiro Takuru was the killer.[%p]
At least, everyone who’d come to the festival to find him seemed to think so.[%p]
And they were probably right.[%p]
The official announcement just said that he was wanted as a person of interest, but if he wasn’t the killer, they wouldn’t have used his real name.[%p]
And she’d also heard that they were, in fact, looking for him as a suspect, though she didn’t know where the information had come from.[%p]
[name]Reporter[line](Miyashiro Takuru... the killer behind the Return of the New Generation Madness.)[%p]
She suddenly felt a rage building inside her.[%p]
There was no way she could do what she’d originally set out to do now. The angel with the heavenly voice was dead.[%p]
But if there was one reason to participate in this festival...[%p]
[name]Reporter[line](That’s right. I don’t care if they’re just rumors. I’ll find all the information on Miyashiro’s whereabouts that I can, and make it easy for people like these to find him.)[%p]
If he wanted to kill himself in public, he was probably some kind of sick madman.[%p]
If he pulled it off, even more idiots might start worshiping him. And that was probably just what he wanted.[%p]
She wouldn’t let that happen. That wouldn’t atone for her murder at all.[%p]
He deserved a miserable, painful death, preferably with people throwing rocks at him like an old historical drama.[%p]
[name]Cameraman[line]“Hey, what’s wrong?”[%p]
[name]Reporter[line]“Oh...”[%p]
Only then did she realize that the people in front of her had stopped talking.[%p]
She said thank you and went to go find another person to interview—[%p]
[name]Reporter[line]“Oh, right. Sorry. One last question?”[%p]
She suddenly stopped, and asked the group one last question she’d thought of before they disappeared into the crowd.[%p]
[name]Reporter[line]“Where are you from?”[%p]
[name]Man[line]“Saitama. We came by bike.”[%p]
Then they laughed again, but she still didn’t get the joke.[%p]
This time she turned around without thanking them, and walked off into the busy street.[%p]
She could tell her cameraman looked confused. He didn’t seem to understand the meaning of her question.[%p]
If they were from Saitama, that meant they weren’t from Shibuya. And from the look of it, they weren’t victims of the earthquake, either.[%p]
People who had nothing to do with the Shibuya Earthquake were here looking for Miyashiro.[%p]
So were the victims of the earthquake she’d just interviewed a while ago, who were hoping that the Restoration Festival would be a success.[%p]
And so were people with a grudge to bear against him, like her.[%p]
[name]Reporter[line](Come on out... Miyashiro.)[%p]
Her thoughts were never going to be heard on TV, and the people nearby couldn’t hear them, either.[%p]
But they were shared by the vast majority of the people at the Restoration Festival.[%p]
Whenever she saw someone wearing a Sumo Sticker on their clothes, she figured they were after Miyashiro. And when she went to interview them, she found that she was right.[%p]
But in fact, the news that Miyashiro was going to kill himself in front of the crowd had been playing on TV every hour since the previous night.[%p]
And because of that, over 98% of the people at the Restoration Festival had heard it.[%p]
There was literally no place safe for him in the city.[%p]
